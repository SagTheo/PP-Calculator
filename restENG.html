Rest

After training and nutrition, rest comes as the third pillar of any good training program. Working out is what will trigger a response from the organism, nutrition will give it the tools, and rest will allow for this whole process to take place. You don't become stronger, faster, more powerful, you don't build muscle during the workout itself. All of that happens when you let your muscles recover.

It is crucial to keep this concept in mind because someone very motivated or impatient to see results will often tend to do too much, hoping to accelerate gains. That does not work here. We stress the muscles, they get repaired, come back stronger, and we repeat this pattern for long enough to actually make results happen.

It is all about perseverance and patience. Trying to take shortcuts will almost always lead to slow progress, frustration, a declining motivation or even giving up, and to top it all up, injuries. Having said that, do not disregard rest. Better, a proper recovery should be a priority, because its benefits go beyond the scope of working out (better focus, more efficient generally, better control over emotions, healthier joints and the list goes on).

Here is some advice on how to optimise recovery:

-Try, as much as possible, to get 7 to 9 hours of sleep per night. Even though this may vary for each individual, this range works well for many.

-Train the same muscle group 2 to 3 times a week(the programs offered here are planned for that frequency).

-Avoid training sore muscles. DOMS(delayed onset muscle soreness) are a sign that the muscle is recovering, and stressing it even more could prevent that recovery or even cause an injury.

-Give your body all the good nutrients it needs.

-Don't forget to work on your flexibility and mobility(see dedicated section). Flexible muscles and mobile joints recover better.

-Listen to your body. If you feel like you need an extra day or two, take them. If a movement causes unusual pain, stop, work on the technique or even choose another exercise. Being strong and in shape is a good thing, but it is even better if it lasts a lifetime, and only a proper recovery that matches the intensity of your training will get you there.

Training

Training makes up one of the three pillars needed to transform your physique as well as acquiring new performances. See training as the reason given to your body to change: the stress that is applied on your muscles during any physical effort triggers a response from them. That response is designed to help you handle that same kind of stress in the future if it were to happen again. This is precisely what allows us to build muscle, become stronger, faster etc.

When wanting to transform your body and/or develop physical abilities, general principles can be applied in order to optimise progression, but also for those who don't exactly know how to structure their training. This is another occasion to remind anyone who is reading this that this is a guide, that will give you said structure. However, I insist on the fact that your training should serve you and your needs, depending on your lifestyle and schedule. Different programs are included here with selected exercises and a structure when it comes to sets, repetitions and training frequency. You can absolutely follow them as they are presented, but don't hesitate to modify certain parameters if need be.

To me, one of the most important characteristics of a good training program is that it should be fun. If you actually enjoy working out, you are on the right track, because it is a very efficient way to maintain the consistency much needed if you wish to achieve your goals.

This idea of having goals is just as important as far as I am concerned. Having a clear goal in mind can sometimes be enough to conjure up motivation to go and train, despite fatigue, personal issues, unexpected events etc. There is one expression that I particularly like that illustrate that mindset: when you know the why, the how does not matter. Of course, it is absolutely fine to practice any physical activity with the sole intention of moving and letting off steam, without necessarily wanting to build a certain body or reach certain performances. Remember that movement is life, moving is then what truly matters.

This section is addressed to those who want to undergo a physical transformation (weight loss and muscle building mainly, although this applies to developing physical abilities as well).

I wish to stay as transparent as possible and not give any false hopes to those who are just beginning in this field. Building muscle, losing fat, becoming stronger, more flexible etc. takes times. Think months and even years, depending also on the body composition you will be starting with.

Not only does it take time, but also riguor, discipline, and consistency. Remember that what brings the most results is what you do the majority of the time, meaning, you won't be able to catch up on one full week of not training in a 3 hour session for example.

To transform your body, you are going to have to recruit it regularly over a long period of time. Besides, you can't just be passive when doing an exercise, you must put in the effort to try and surpass yourself everytime, goal being to attain a high enough intensity. Your body is a particularly evolved and intelligent machine. It preserves itself permanently as a way to spend as less energy as possible and execute any given action in the most efficient way possible. That is precisely the reason why you have to give it a reason to change, and you get there through intense and repeated efforts, over a long period of time.

So, the best advice I can give you is to enjoy every single small evolution, every single step made towards your goal. Take pleasure engaging yourself in this changing process, persevere, and results will come. This is not an easy endeavour, but the benefits you will reap from it go way beyond the difficulties you will encounter.

During a training session, when stress is being applied on muscles, you create microtears on your muscle fibers. Those microtears trigger a defense mechanism: your body will repair them and build your muscle fibers back up thicker, as a way to be more prepared for a possible future stress of that kind.

That recovery will occur when you will be resting, whilst sleeping or when not under the type of effort that caused the microtears. That process can be maximised if you provide your body with the necessary tools, meaning good nutrients. Hence the importance of eating and resting properly, and training intensely enough as said earlier.

Any proper training session should start with a warm-up. It is not the most exciting part, but it is crucial to me. Warming-up, as you can tell by the name, allows for the body, joints, tendons and ligaments to get warm and ready for whatever you are about to recruit them for.

More than just a physical preparation, it is also a mental preparation: I like seeing the warm-up as a clear cut between your daily life and your training session. This introduction phase allows you to be more focused and present to optimise the efficiency of your workout.

Warming-up well is also one of the best ways to prevent injuries from happening. Blood will flow through the muscles, improving their oxygenation during effort. Tendons, though they are particularly resilient, will be better prepared to handle deep ranges of motion and whatever resistance will be applied on them. Joints will be better lubricated and will drive movement more smoothly. Your strength and flexibility are not necessarily available on demand, and usually need some warming-up to be unlocked fully.

Here is an example of a warm-up routine that I use and that keeps me away from injuries. It takes about fifteen minutes:

Phase 1 : cardio-vascular exercise, to get your heart rate to rise and a nice blood flow, and it literally warms up the body. I particularly like jump roping, that I do for 3 to 4 minutes.
Phase 2 : dynamic stretches, to unlock your joints and wake your muscles and tendons up. Keep in mind that it should be dynamic stretches: each position is held for a few seconds only, as holding them for too long would defeat the purpose of warming up and make you less sharp physically.
Phase 3 : specific warm-up. That is where you prime the area that you will work on that day. In order to do that, you can perform repetitions of an exercise that recruits those muscles you want to train, at a low intensity that is, to really get them and the associated joints ready. Elastic bands can come in really handy here: they are perfect to create blood flow safely, without creating too much fatigue, and that is what you want. You shouldn't finish a warm-up drained of your energy, to the contrary, you should feel energised and ready.

Shoulder positionning plays a crucial role when it comes to bodyweight training. Any upper body moves you will perform will require a specific shoulder position. I believe it is then relevant to talk about this topic, as a way to convey the importance of proper execution and keep injuries at bay as much as possible.
There are 4 different shoulder positions: protraction, retraction, depression and elevation.

  • protraction: the shoulder blades are pushed forward, getting away from each other. This position is mainly used during pushing movements (handstand push-ups, push-ups, planche etc.).
  • retraction: the opposite to protraction. The shoulder blades are pulled back , getting closer to one another. Pulling movements require this position (bodyweight rows for example).
  • elevation: the shoulder blades are raised towards the ears just like when you shrug your shoulders. It recruits mainly the upper traps. This is the position you want to use when doing a handstand.
  • depression: the opposite to elevation. The shoulder blades are pulled down. This position is particularly stable for the shoulders and therefore safe for the whole joint. Almost all movements should be performed with the shoulders depressed (pull-ups, push-ups, bodyweight rows, dips etc.).

Of course, the shoulder is a very mobile joint, as a consequence, it will never stay in one position during the execution of a movement(except when working on isometric exercises where the position is held at one given angle). I do believe however that it is important to get comfortable with protracting, retracting, elevating and depressing the shoulders in order to develop better control over this joint and the exercises that recruit it.

The core deserves some attention. When I talk about the core, I mean the abs of course, but also the obliques, the transversus abdominis(a deep ab muscle), and the lower back.

That muscle group has many roles:
-maintaining posture
-maintaining the vital organs safe in the abdomen
-it is a natural belt that protects your lower back
-preventing spinal extension
-preventing rotation
-transfering force between the lower and upper body

Having a strong, resilient and endurant core is then beneficial not only to your training but also to your overall health and daily activities.

When it comes to training it, opinions diverge. Some will include specific core work within their sessions, whilst others consider doing compound movements such as push-ups, pull-ups and squats for example is enough of a stimulus to train that area.

Personally, I do not train my core directly. My training sessions are made up of bodyweight exercises only, and as a result, my core is constantly being recruited. I do however include some lower-back work to strengthen it, given that it is often an area that gets left out when doing calisthenics. As said earlier, your training, your needs.

It is then on you to decide whether you should target that area or not. For those who are starting though, I would advise you include some specific core work, and adapt consequently as you get to know your body and your needs.

So, here are three circuits that won't take too long to complete and that target each part of the core:

You may include this core work at the very beginning or at the very end of a session, or if you would like, during your rest days.
3 times a week is a good frequency, but you may also work on it everyday, given that those muscles recover quite quickly.

If you are interested with bodyweight training, chances are that being able to stand on your hands is one of your goals. This skill requires balance, proprioception(the ability to know where your body is in space), mobility, and a certain level of strength, especially shoulder strength. Being able to hold the handstand will introduce you to a plethora of new exercises: handstand push-up, 90 degree handstand push-up, straigth-arm press to handstand, planche, one-arm handstand etc. So, here is a guide of the essential to know in order to achieve a handstand.

Standing on your hands requires some shoulder, triceps and forearm strength. To develop that strength, work on push-ups. I would advise being able to do at least 5 push-ups with proper technique.

Being upside down can be uncomfortable as it is not a natural position. To get used to it, practice the tripod (you balance yourself upside down with your head and your hands). That will also develop your ability to align your body and will strengthen your core.

The wrists are not used to bare the entire weight of the body. Make sure to warm them up properly and strengthen the forearm flexors and extensors.

The handstand demands good shoulder mobility, especially if you want to hold it nice and straight. In order to do that, you should be able to raise your arms above your head without having to arch your back. If that is impossible for you, work on mobility exercises that put you in that very position (dead hangs for example, the back bridge is an excellent one as well).

Your core will come into play to maintain not only balance but also the alignment of your body from head to toe. Strengthen it if needed with exercises such as the superman and the reverse plank (isometric exercises are a better choice here as the handstand is itself a static skill).

The wall is your best friend for this skill. Spend time upside down back to the wall first then, when you feel more confident, chest to the wall (promotes better alignment). As you progress, start taking your feet off of the wall and try and find your balance. Ideally, you should be able to hold a handstand using the wall for at least 30 seconds, even 1 minute, to build enough stamina. It is good to be able to do a hanstand, but if your shoulders give up after a few seconds only, you won't be able to do much with it.

Remember that unlocking this skill is all about patience and consistency. I advise you pratice it everyday for about 10 minutes.
The aim is not to exhaust yourself practicing it so often, but rather to recruit your body and your brain in that position frequently enough so that it becomes second nature.
Use the wall as said earlier, but don't forget to also spend some time playing with free handstands.
When you will be able to hold it, you may even go from 10 to 5 minutes of practice everyday if you wish, as at that point, it will be mainly about maintaining that newly-acquired ability.

Let's start by making the difference between mobility and flexibility. Flexibility is the ability for a muscle to lengthen and get to a certain range of motion using gravity or an external force. Mobility is closely related to flexibility, as the muscle has to lengthen as well, however strength comes into play here. Someone mobile will then be able to generate force in a stretched position, and won't need gravity or assistance to get to said position.

Let's take an example to illustrate this difference. I can do the side splits, and I can get to that position by letting gravity push me all the way to the ground, legs straight on each side: I have enough flexibility to perform that movement. Now, if I were to hold that very position but between two chairs, each foot on a chair with the rest of my body not being supported, I wouldn't be able to do it, simply because I don't possess that kind of mobility. In other words, my adductors are flexible enough to do the side splits but not strong enough to generate strength in that range of motion.

Someone flexible, then, is not necessarily mobile, but someone mobile most definitely possesses a good level of flexibility.

I wanted to make the difference because I believe that it is more relevant to focus on mobility rather than just on flexibility. Also, even though being flexible is important for better recovery and to avoid pain and injuries, being mobile will only multiply these benefits. I have also noticed that good mobility allows to reach deep ranges of motion faster, meaning the body needs little to no preparation to get to the desired stretched position.

Here is a list of exercises that target overall flexibility, and are in my opinion the most important to steer clear from injuries and feel good in your body. As explained earlier, mobility being more relevant to work on, some of the following exercises are more mobility-focused and vice-versa.

How to train flexibility/mobility

If you want, you can dedicate entire workouts to flexibility and mobility. This is especially true for advanced exercises that require more preparation such as the splits.

However, one method that I particularly enjoy doing myself is to stretch regularly throughout the day. Whenever you can, bend over and try and touch your toes with your legs straight. If you have access to a bar or somewhere to hang from, perform a few repetitions of the skin the cat exercise, or if that is too difficult, simply hang from the bar. I use dynamic stretches at the beginning of my sessions and passive stretches at the end, and that is another way to include some flexibility and mobility work.

The most important thing is to recruit your body in deep ranges of motion often enough, this way your muscles and joints will get used to it. Note that just using a full range of motion during the execution of the exercises of your workouts already contributes to improving your mobility.

Don't skip on flexibility and mobility work, because again, the benefits go so much beyond the scope of training and performance.

NB: I have included the splits in the list above. Depending on your hip anatomy, it is possible that this movement(the side splits) may not be feasible for you, at least not the version where your legs form a 180 degree angle. This exercise remains relevant to strengthen the adductors and develop good hip mobility, but don't force your way down if you feel a lot of pressure in your hips. Work on it until your reach the position that your anatomy will let you achieve, and once again, listen to your body.
